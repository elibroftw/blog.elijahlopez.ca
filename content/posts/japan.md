## Life Before Japan

Before Japan, for over a decade, I mistakenly thought I was an introvert. This couldn't be further from the truth. I'm an extrovert PLUS PLUS. For the last decade+, this extroverted nature of mine has been suppressed by my environment, by those I call my friends (surrounded by introverts), by the very nature of the high school I went to, to the very nature of the minimal socialization time I had with other people at school, to the very nature of me trusting people had my best interests at heart. It was all a lie. I'll tell you who I used to be before high school.

Before high school I would talk to random girls and get their numbers and then text both of them simultaneously while knowing full well they were friends with each other. LOL. That's who I used to be. It all went downhill after the first week of high school. I've blacked out as much of high school and university as I can. What a mess. A decade of my life lived incorrectly. It's all good though, as it has shaped me to protect MYSELF first and foremost, and enjoy being indifferent to what goes around.

## How is this a Preface

PREFACE: I do not like traveling. But Japan? Japan was amazing. When people talk about diversity of Toronto, the diversity of tourists in Japan is how high the bar should be. I love being my authentic self, not being held back, and being able to start conversations with basically everyone who can speak English.

In Canada? Extroversion isn't looked down upon per se, but it isn't encouraged at all in a social setting.

Just recently, I was out with a friend getting ice cream at a parlor when two women walked in. They looked at me, and I looked at them. However, because they were just about to order, I didn't want to interfere, so I didn't say anything right then.

When my friend and I were walking outside afterward, I was explaining this exact concept to him. I told him, "I only held back back there because interrupting someone's order is rude."

Lo and behold, as my friend and I were walking back, the two women were walking our direction. Naturally, I took it as the perfect opportunity to strike up a conversation. I said, "Hey, we saw you at the ice cream parlor. How is it?" The blonde woman smiled and replied, "Yum, yum!" in a upbeat tone. I laughed, and we continued on our separate ways.

Afterward, my friend told me I should give him a heads-up next time. Honestly, that made no sense to me. Not only had I done something similar before with a friend when I was in Japan (to some girls walking behind us), but I had literally just foreshadowed the entire interaction when I told him, "If it wasn't for the potential to interfere with their ordering..."

He claimed he could have assisted if he had known, but to be completely honest, there are plenty of people in a city to socialize with. The fun of socializing is really just about reading and picking up on body language cues to bail out. When you make a casual comment, the other person has a choice: they can either stop to continue the conversation, or they can provide a quick quip. In the latter case, you simply read the cue and continue on your MERRY way.

## Japan is Inspiring

In Japan, the parks were lively. Kids laughing joyously in the park. I swear I've never heard such pleasant to the ears background noise while reading.

Talking to people is so easy. Just say hi, and they are socially lubricated. Alcohol isn't for cliques, it's for socializing.

Japan is inspiring. I walked into a bookstore and saw an art piece and I bought it on the spot. Now I have a new hobby, visiting art galleries where you can purchase art. I will be spending this year implementing my optimisms.

The less I say the better, in real life is where this type of conversation belongs.

So go to Japan if you haven't. I will try to exhume that sort of energy in Canada, but will be back in Japan if I cannot make it work here.
